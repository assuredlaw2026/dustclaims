The Site

A 226-Acre Corridor
Through the Neighborhood

Clark County Air Quality dust permit 58333, held by Las Vegas Paving Corp. for the “Boulder Reimagine” project, covers 226.51 acres running the length of the Boulder Highway corridor in Las Vegas, directly alongside homes and businesses.

Why This Site Matters

226 Acres Alongside
Homes and Businesses

At 226 acres, the Boulder Reimagine project is far larger than the minimum acreage requiring a Dust Control Operating Permit, and large enough to require mandatory on-site Dust Control Monitoring under Clark County Air Quality Regulations. The following standards apply to every day of active construction.

226

Acres of Disturbed Soil

Projects disturbing 0.25 acres or more are required to obtain a Dust Control Operating Permit. At 226 acres, this project is hundreds of times that threshold, and the potential dust impact on the surrounding corridor is significant.

CC AQR Section 94.4.1(b)(1)
DCM

Dust Control Monitor Required

Any construction project with 50 acres or more of disturbed soil at any given time is required to have a certified Dust Control Monitor present during all construction activities. This site triggers that requirement many times over.

CC AQR Section 94.8(a)(1)
100ft

No Dust Across Property Lines

Regardless of permit status, no person engaged in construction activities may allow visible emissions of particulate matter to cross a property line or extend more than 100 feet. Residents and businesses along this corridor have enforceable rights if this occurs.

CC AQR Section 94.14(a)(3),(4)

If You Live Nearby

What to Watch For

If you live, work, or own property along the Boulder Highway corridor near this project, here is what constitutes a reportable violation under Clark County Air Quality Regulations. Document anything you observe and contact Assured Law.

Visible Dust Clouds

Any visible dust plume extending beyond the site boundary or crossing your property line is a potential violation. Document with video or photos, showing the source and the plume reaching your property.

After-Hours Construction

Construction activity, equipment operation, or material deliveries outside of permitted hours is a violation. Note the exact time and take video if safe to do so.

Equipment on Public Streets

Heavy construction equipment operating on public streets without visible traffic control measures or flaggers may be unauthorized. Photograph the equipment type and street location.

No Water Truck at Start of Day

Clark County regulations require dust control measures to be in place before construction begins each day. Grading, earthmoving, or excavation starting without water application is a potential violation.

Trackout on Public Roads

Mud, dirt, or construction debris tracked from the site onto Boulder Highway or any adjacent street is a violation. Photograph the deposit and its origin point.

Pool, HVAC, or Surface Contamination

Fine sediment in your pool, on your AC unit and filters, or coating exterior surfaces may be attributable to nearby construction activity. Document with dated photographs before cleaning.

Your Rights as a Neighbor

What the Law
Protects

Whether or not any agency acts, you have private enforcement rights under Nevada law and Clark County Code. A permit does not give a developer the right to damage your property or your health.

Property Rights
Dust crossing your property line without authorization is a trespass and a nuisance under Nevada common law, regardless of whether a permit is in place.
Health Damages
Health impacts arising from airborne particulate matter attributable to construction activity on the site are compensable damages in a private action.
Property Damage
Costs of cleaning pools, replacing HVAC filters, repairing exterior surfaces, and any other repairs necessitated by construction dust are recoverable.
Injunctive Relief
Courts can order construction to cease or comply with applicable standards within days of filing. Government inaction does not eliminate this option.
